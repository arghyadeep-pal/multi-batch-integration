1. Quality Control (QC) & Filtering What: Removing "bad" cells (e.g., those with >20% mitochondrial DNA, indicating cell death) and "bad" genes (e.g., expressed in <3 cells). Why: If you include dead cells, your clustering will show a "dead cell cluster" that has no biological meaning and ruins your statistics. When not to use: If your specific tissue naturally has high mitochondrial activity (like heart or muscle cells), strict QC might accidentally delete your target population.
2. Normalization What: Adjusting counts so every cell has the same "total count" (e.g., 10,000). Why: Cell A might have 5,000 detected molecules and Cell B might have 2,000 simply because the sequencer worked better on Cell A. Normalization makes them comparable. Methods: CPM (Total Count): Simple and fast. SCTransform: More complex; it models noise specifically for each gene. You use this if your data is very "noisy" or has deep sequencing depth.
3. Data Transformation (Log1p) What: Calculating for every value. Why: Gene expression is often "skewed"—a few genes have massive counts while most have tiny counts. Log-transformation pulls these outliers in, making the data look more like a "normal distribution" which most algorithms (like PCA) require.
4. Scaling (Z-score) What: Shifting the mean of every gene to 0 and variance to 1. Why: Without scaling, a highly expressed gene (e.g., ACTB) will look "more important" to the computer than a lowly expressed master-regulator gene, even if the lowly expressed one is the one changing between clusters. When not to use: Some researchers skip scaling if they want to preserve the absolute magnitude of gene differences, but it is standard for Leiden clustering.
5. Batch Effect Correction What: Merging data from two different labs or days. Why: Often, the "Day 1" cells look different from "Day 2" cells just because of the room temperature or the person holding the pipette. When to use: Only if you have multiple datasets. Do not use if you have one single run, as you might "correct away" real biology.

Can we use all types at the same time? Yes—and in fact, a standard pipeline usually includes almost all of them. However, they must be applied in a specific linear order. Using them out of order (e.g., scaling before normalizing) will give you mathematically invalid results. The Standard "Gold Standard" Order: QC Filtering (Clean the trash) Normalization (Equalize the cells) Log Transformation (Compress the range) Feature Selection (Keep only interesting genes) Scaling (Equalize gene importance) Batch Correction (Merge datasets if needed) Why we choose one over another? The choice often depends on the sparsity of the data: Simple Library Normalization is used when you want a quick, interpretable look at the data. SCTransform (Normalization + Scaling in one) is used when you have very high-quality data and want the most precise mathematical model, even if it's slower to compute.
